Introduction

What Is the Fake & Real News Dataset?

The Fake and Real News Dataset, curated by Clément Bisaillon and hosted on Kaggle, is a widely-used binary classification corpus consisting of 44,898 full-length English news articles divided into two CSV files — Fake.csv and True.csv. It has become one of the most downloaded fake news datasets on Kaggle, with thousands of notebooks, research papers, and student projects built on top of it.

The real news articles are sourced primarily from Reuters.com, one of the world's most respected wire services, providing a gold standard of journalistic rigour. The fake news articles were collected from sources flagged as unreliable by Politifact and Wikipedia, comprising fabricated, misleading, or heavily biased content. Each article includes a title, full body text, a subject category, and a publication date.

Unlike the LIAR dataset's six-point veracity scale, this corpus offers a clean binary split — making it ideal as a first benchmark for NLP students and practitioners entering the fake news detection space. However, this very simplicity also introduces significant methodological pitfalls that researchers must carefully navigate.

Why This Dataset Matters

This is the most accessible large-scale fake news dataset available. Its size (44K+ articles), clean formatting (CSV with headers), full article text (not just headlines or single sentences), and binary labelling make it the de facto starting point for binary fake news classification benchmarks. But its ease of use masks critical data leakage risks — particularly the Reuters byline problem — which every serious researcher must address.

Label Taxonomy

Binary Classification — Fake vs. Real

Unlike multi-class veracity datasets, this corpus uses a hard binary partition. Each article is either entirely Fake (sourced from unreliable outlets) or entirely Real (sourced from Reuters). There is no middle ground, no "half-true," no gradation. This makes the task conceptually simpler — but introduces source-level bias, since the label is determined by the outlet, not by per-claim fact-checking.

Critical Warning — Source-Level Labelling Bias

Labels in this dataset are assigned at the source level, not the claim level. Every article from Reuters is labelled "Real" and every article from flagged outlets is labelled "Fake." This means a model can achieve near-perfect accuracy by learning stylistic artefacts (e.g. Reuters dateline format: "WASHINGTON (Reuters) –") rather than actual veracity signals. Always strip source-identifying metadata before training.

Data Schema

4-Column CSV Structure

The dataset is distributed as two CSV files with headers. The schema is minimal — just four columns — but the text field contains complete article bodies, often spanning hundreds of words. The subject field uses different category taxonomies for fake and real news, which is itself a leakage vector.

# Field Name Type Description Example
0 title string Headline of the news article "Trump Launches Fresh Attack on Courts..."
1 text string MAIN FEATURE Full article body text (often 200–1500+ words). Real articles often begin with Reuters byline prefix. "WASHINGTON (Reuters) - President Donald Trump..."
2 subject categorical Topic category. Different taxonomies are used for fake vs. real articles — this itself is a leakage signal. politicsNews
3 date string Publication date (format varies: "December 31, 2017" or similar). Some entries are malformed or missing. December 31, 2017
— label binary TARGET Derived from filename. Not present as a column — inferred from which CSV file the row belongs to (Fake.csv → 0, True.csv → 1). 0 or 1
Subject Field — Hidden Leakage

The subject column uses entirely different categories for fake and real articles. Real articles use politicsNews and worldnews. Fake articles use News, politics, Government News, left-news, US_News, and Middle-east. A model trained on subject alone would achieve >90% accuracy. Drop this column or use it only for analysis, never as a training feature.

5
Critical Issues

Data Leakage & The Reuters Byline Problem

The single most important methodological concern with this dataset is data leakage through source-identifying artefacts. Nearly every "Real" article begins with a Reuters-format dateline — CITY_NAME (Reuters) - — that instantly reveals its class membership. A trivial regex achieves 99%+ accuracy, rendering any model that does not strip this prefix useless as a genuine fake news detector.

The Reuters Byline — Mandatory Preprocessing

Approximately 96% of real articles contain the substring "(Reuters)" in their first 50 characters. Zero fake articles contain this string. A single-feature logistic regression on the boolean has_reuters = "(Reuters)" in text[:50] yields ~96% accuracy. Any reported accuracy above ~95% on this dataset without byline stripping is measuring source detection, not fake news detection.

⚠1
Reuters Dateline Leakage
Real articles start with "CITY (Reuters) - " prefix. Strip this with regex r'^[A-Z\s]+(Reuters)\s*[-–—]\s*' before any text processing. Also remove trailing Reuters boilerplate.
⚠2
Subject Column Leakage
Fake and real articles use completely disjoint subject taxonomies. The subject field alone gives >90% accuracy. Never use it as a training feature — analyse it for understanding, but drop it for modelling.
⚠3
Source Homogeneity
All real articles come from one source (Reuters). Models learn Reuters house style rather than "truth." Evaluation on non-Reuters real articles will show severe performance drops. This is a single-source benchmark, not a general detector.
⚠4
No Pre-Defined Split
Unlike LIAR, this dataset has no official train/val/test split. Always use stratified splitting and report your split ratio. Random splitting risks temporal leakage — consider chronological splits for realism.
6
Implementation Guide

Loading, Cleaning & Modelling Pipeline

The pipeline below demonstrates proper loading, the critical byline-stripping step, and both a classical ML baseline (TF-IDF + Logistic Regression) and a transformer fine-tuning approach. Note the emphasis on cleaning before any feature extraction — the order matters.

01
Load & Label
Read both CSVs. Add a label column (Fake.csv → 0, True.csv → 1). Concatenate into a single DataFrame. Drop any rows with null text.
02
Strip Source Artefacts
Remove Reuters dateline prefix via regex. Remove trailing boilerplate. Optionally strip all named entity references to test content-only signal strength.
03
Stratified Split
80/10/10 train/val/test with stratification on label. For temporal experiments, parse dates and split chronologically. Always report your exact split method.
04
Model Training
Baseline: TF-IDF (max 50K features, 1-2 grams) → LogisticRegression(C=1.0). Advanced: BERT/DistilBERT fine-tuning with truncation at 512 tokens. Report accuracy, F1, precision, recall.
# Fake & Real News Dataset — Complete Loading & Cleaning Pipeline
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import re
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split

# ── Step 1: Load both CSV files and assign labels ──
fake = pd.read_csv("Fake.csv")
real = pd.read_csv("True.csv")

fake["label"] = 0   # Fake → 0
real["label"] = 1   # Real → 1

df = pd.concat([fake, real], ignore_index=True)
df = df.dropna(subset=["text"])
df = df.sample(frac=1, random_state=42).reset_index(drop=True)

print(f"Total articles: {len(df)}")
print(df["label"].value_counts())

# ── Step 2: CRITICAL — Strip Reuters dateline leakage ──
def clean_text(text):
    # Remove Reuters dateline: "CITY (Reuters) - "
    text = re.sub(r'^[A-Z\s,.]{{0,60}}\(Reuters\)\s*[-–—]\s*', '', text)
    # Remove trailing Reuters boilerplate
    text = re.sub(r'\s*\(Reporting by.*$', '', text, flags=re.DOTALL)
    text = re.sub(r'\s*\(Additional reporting.*$', '', text, flags=re.DOTALL)
    text = re.sub(r'\s*\(Editing by.*$', '', text, flags=re.DOTALL)
    return text.strip()

df["text_clean"] = df["text"].apply(clean_text)

# Verify leakage is removed
reuters_check = df[df["text_clean"].str.contains("(Reuters)", regex=False)]
print(f"Residual Reuters mentions: {len(reuters_check)}")

# ── Step 3: Stratified train/val/test split ──
train, temp = train_test_split(df, test_size=0.2, stratify=df["label"], random_state=42)
val, test   = train_test_split(temp, test_size=0.5, stratify=temp["label"], random_state=42)

print(f"Train: {len(train)} | Val: {len(val)} | Test: {len(test)}")
# → Train: 35,918 | Val: 4,490 | Test: 4,490
# TF-IDF + Logistic Regression Baseline (after cleaning)
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.metrics import classification_report, accuracy_score

tfidf = TfidfVectorizer(
    max_features=50000,
    ngram_range=(1, 2),
    min_df=3,
    max_df=0.95,
    sublinear_tf=True
)

X_train = tfidf.fit_transform(train["text_clean"])
X_val   = tfidf.transform(val["text_clean"])
X_test  = tfidf.transform(test["text_clean"])

clf = LogisticRegression(C=1.0, max_iter=1000, solver="lbfgs")
clf.fit(X_train, train["label"])

y_pred = clf.predict(X_test)
print(f"Test Accuracy (cleaned): {accuracy_score(test['label'], y_pred):.4f}")
print(classification_report(test["label"], y_pred,
      target_names=["Fake", "Real"]))
# BERT / DistilBERT Fine-Tuning Pipeline
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForSequenceClassification
from transformers import Trainer, TrainingArguments
from datasets import Dataset

MODEL = "distilbert-base-uncased"
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(MODEL)

def tokenise(batch):
    return tokenizer(
        batch["text_clean"],
        truncation=True,
        padding="max_length",
        max_length=512
    )

# Convert to HuggingFace Datasets
hf_train = Dataset.from_pandas(train[["text_clean", "label"]].rename(
    columns={"label": "labels"}))
hf_val   = Dataset.from_pandas(val[["text_clean", "label"]].rename(
    columns={"label": "labels"}))
hf_test  = Dataset.from_pandas(test[["text_clean", "label"]].rename(
    columns={"label": "labels"}))

hf_train = hf_train.map(tokenise, batched=True)
hf_val   = hf_val.map(tokenise, batched=True)
hf_test  = hf_test.map(tokenise, batched=True)

model = AutoModelForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained(MODEL, num_labels=2)

args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="./fnd-distilbert",
    num_train_epochs=3,
    per_device_train_batch_size=16,
    per_device_eval_batch_size=32,
    learning_rate=2e-5,
    evaluation_strategy="epoch",
    save_strategy="epoch",
    load_best_model_at_end=True,
    metric_for_best_model="eval_loss",
    warmup_ratio=0.06,
    weight_decay=0.01,
    fp16=True,
)

trainer = Trainer(
    model=model, args=args,
    train_dataset=hf_train, eval_dataset=hf_val,
)
trainer.train()
results = trainer.evaluate(hf_test)
print(results)

Interpreting Benchmark Results

The suspiciously high accuracy (>95%) even after cleaning suggests that stylistic differences between Reuters wire copy and fabricated content remain a dominant signal. This dataset benchmarks source-style classification more than factual veracity detection. For genuine fake news detection research, pair this dataset with cross-source evaluation — train here, test on articles from a completely different source distribution.

Limitations & Ethics

What This Dataset Cannot Tell You

!
Single-Source Real News Bias
All real articles come from Reuters. Models learn Reuters style, not "truth." Legitimate journalism from the NYT, BBC, AP, or local papers may be misclassified as fake because it doesn't match the Reuters template. Never deploy a model trained solely on this data.
!
Temporal & Political Bias
The dataset covers ~2015–2018, a period dominated by US presidential politics (Trump, Clinton, Obama). Models will underperform on non-US political content, non-political news, or post-2018 events. Treat this as a closed temporal benchmark.
!
No Claim-Level Verification
Labels are source-level, not claim-level. A Reuters article containing an error is still labelled "Real." A fabricated article accidentally containing a true claim is still "Fake." This conflates source reliability with factual accuracy — a critical distinction in real-world fact-checking.
!
Ethical Deployment Risk
Deploying a classifier trained on this dataset in production would mislabel legitimate independent journalism as "fake" and potentially pass AI-generated content that mimics Reuters style as "real." This dataset is for research and education only — never for production content moderation.

Spot the Pattern

Notice how fake articles use ALL-CAPS in headlines, sensational language ("BREAKING," "EXPOSED," "SICK"), emotional appeals, and anonymous sourcing. Real articles use neutral headlines, Reuters dateline prefixes, named sources, and inverted-pyramid structure. These stylistic signals are what models learn — and they generalise poorly to sophisticated modern misinformation that mimics legitimate journalism's style.
