Introduction

What Is the LIAR Dataset?

The LIAR dataset, introduced by William Yang Wang at EMNLP 2017, represents a fundamental rethinking of how fake news detection should be framed. Rather than the binary real/fake split used by contemporaneous datasets, LIAR maps political statement veracity along a six-point scale drawn from a decade of expert human fact-checking — transforming what was a classification task into something closer to truthfulness grading.

Every statement in LIAR was sourced from PolitiFact.com, a Pulitzer Prize–winning fact-checking organisation that has been rating political claims since 2007. Each PolitiFact entry involves journalist investigation, source verification, and editor review before a verdict is rendered. LIAR aggregates 12,836 of these verdicts, along with the full contextual metadata attached to each — speaker identity, speaker history, statement context, party affiliation — creating the richest per-statement metadata of any publicly available fake news corpus.

Why LIAR Changed the Field

Before LIAR, most fake news datasets treated truth as binary. Wang's key insight was that truthfulness is a spectrum — a claim can be misleading without being outright false, or technically accurate while being deeply deceptive in context. The six-class formulation forces models to learn gradations of veracity, not just polarity. This makes LIAR significantly harder — but also significantly more realistic — than binary alternatives.

Label Taxonomy

The Six-Point Verdict Scale

PolitiFact's six labels are not arbitrary — they represent a carefully calibrated spectrum of truthfulness developed by professional journalists over a decade of political fact-checking. Understanding each label's precise semantic meaning is essential for correct model evaluation and avoidance of adjacent-class errors.

Adjacent-Class Error Interpretation

Confusing barely-true with half-true is semantically much less severe than confusing pants-fire with true. Always report ordinal evaluation metrics (mean absolute error, off-by-one accuracy) alongside standard accuracy. A model that achieves 30% exact accuracy but never makes errors of distance >1 is substantially better than one making extreme label confusions.

Data Schema

14-Field TSV Structure

The LIAR dataset is distributed as three tab-separated TSV files (train.tsv, valid.tsv, test.tsv), each with 14 columns and no header row. The schema's richness is its greatest asset — and its complexity its greatest trap for first-time users who skip the column index mapping.

Speaker Credibility

The Credit History Vector

The credit history vector — five integer counts representing a speaker's entire PolitiFact verdict history — is LIAR's most distinctive and powerful feature. It encodes a prior probability distribution over that speaker's future truthfulness. A politician who has accumulated 200 "false" and "pants-fire" ratings is statistically more likely to be lying than one with 200 "true" and "mostly-true" ratings. This Bayesian signal is complementary to the linguistic content of each statement.

Wang's original paper showed that a simple logistic regression on the credit history vector alone achieves approximately 20% accuracy on the 6-class task — nearly matching LSTM baselines that use only statement text. When fused together (text + speaker credit history), the combined model consistently outperforms either signal alone.

Implementation Note — Credit History Leakage

The credit history vector is accumulated over time on PolitiFact. For a chronologically correct split, ensure that test statements appear temporally after training statements — otherwise you risk using future verdict counts to predict current labels. Wang's original split does not guarantee temporal ordering, so chronological data splitting is an open research hygiene issue for this dataset.

Implementation Guide

Loading, Preprocessing & Modelling

The LIAR dataset requires careful column-index mapping (no headers in TSV), label encoding, and metadata fusion strategy. The pipeline below covers download, loading, feature engineering for the credit history vector, and BERT fine-tuning with metadata injection.

01
Download & Parse TSV
Three headerless TSV files. Assign column names manually from the 14-field schema. Verify column count per row — a few records contain tab characters in the statement field and will misparse.
02
Label Encoding
Map string labels to ordered integers (pants-fire→0, false→1, barely-true→2, half-true→3, mostly-true→4, true→5). For binary tasks, collapse to {0,1,2} = fake, {3,4,5} = real — or use ≤1 as fake threshold.
03
Credit Vector Feature
Normalise five credit counts (cols 8–12) per speaker. Compute: total = sum(counts); proportion vector = counts/total. Add a "speaker_seen" boolean for test-time speakers absent from training data.
04
Fusion Strategy
Three options: (a) late fusion — separate encoders for text and metadata, concatenate before classification head; (b) prompt injection — prepend metadata as text tokens; (c) auxiliary loss on metadata only.
# LIAR Dataset — Complete Loading & Feature Engineering Pipeline
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

COLUMNS = [
    "id", "label", "statement", "subject", "speaker",
    "speaker_job", "state", "party",
    "barely_true_c", "false_c", "half_true_c",
    "mostly_true_c", "pants_fire_c", "context"
]

LABEL_MAP = {
    "pants-fire": 0, "false": 1, "barely-true": 2,
    "half-true": 3, "mostly-true": 4, "true": 5
}

CREDIT_COLS = ["barely_true_c", "false_c", "half_true_c",
               "mostly_true_c", "pants_fire_c"]

def load_liar(split_path: str) -> pd.DataFrame:
    df = pd.read_csv(split_path, sep="\t", header=None,
                     names=COLUMNS, dtype=str)
    df["label_int"] = df["label"].map(LABEL_MAP)
    # binary: 0/1/2 = fake, 3/4/5 = real
    df["label_binary"] = (df["label_int"] >= 3).astype(int)
    # credit history — convert to numeric, fill missing with 0
    for c in CREDIT_COLS:
        df[c] = pd.to_numeric(df[c], errors="coerce").fillna(0)
    df["credit_total"]   = df[CREDIT_COLS].sum(axis=1).clip(lower=1)
    df["credit_vector"]  = df[CREDIT_COLS].values.tolist()
    df["credit_norm"]    = df[CREDIT_COLS].div(df["credit_total"], axis=0).values.tolist()
    # simple credibility prior: (mostly_true + true) / total
    df["credibility"] = (df["mostly_true_c"] + 0) / df["credit_total"]
    return df

train = load_liar("./liar_dataset/train.tsv")
valid = load_liar("./liar_dataset/valid.tsv")
test  = load_liar("./liar_dataset/test.tsv")

print(train["label"].value_counts().sort_index())
print(f"Unique speakers in train: {train['speaker'].nunique()}")
# BERT fine-tuning with metadata injection (prompt-based fusion)
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForSequenceClassification
from transformers import Trainer, TrainingArguments
from datasets import Dataset
import torch

MODEL = "bert-base-uncased"
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(MODEL)

def build_input(row):
    # inject metadata as prefix tokens before statement
    meta = (
        f"Speaker: {row['speaker'] or 'unknown'}. "
        f"Party: {row['party'] or 'unknown'}. "
        f"Context: {row['context'] or 'unknown'}. "
        f"Claim: {row['statement']}"
    )
    return meta

train["input_text"] = train.apply(build_input, axis=1)
valid["input_text"] = valid.apply(build_input, axis=1)
test["input_text"]  = test.apply(build_input,  axis=1)

def tokenise(batch):
    return tokenizer(batch["input_text"],
                     truncation=True, padding="max_length",
                     max_length=256)

hf_train = Dataset.from_pandas(train[["input_text","label_int"]].rename(columns={"label_int":"labels"}))
hf_valid = Dataset.from_pandas(valid[["input_text","label_int"]].rename(columns={"label_int":"labels"}))
hf_test  = Dataset.from_pandas(test[["input_text","label_int"]].rename(columns={"label_int":"labels"}))

hf_train = hf_train.map(tokenise, batched=True)
hf_valid = hf_valid.map(tokenise, batched=True)
hf_test  = hf_test.map(tokenise,  batched=True)

model = AutoModelForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained(MODEL, num_labels=6)

# compute class weights to handle mild imbalance
counts = train["label_int"].value_counts().sort_index()
weights = torch.tensor(1.0 / counts.values, dtype=torch.float32)
weights = weights / weights.sum()

class WeightedTrainer(Trainer):
    def compute_loss(self, model, inputs, return_outputs=False, **kw):
        labels  = inputs.pop("labels")
        outputs = model(**inputs)
        loss_fn = torch.nn.CrossEntropyLoss(weight=weights.to(model.device))
        loss = loss_fn(outputs.logits, labels)
        return (loss, outputs) if return_outputs else loss

args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="./liar-bert",
    num_train_epochs=5,
    per_device_train_batch_size=32,
    learning_rate=2e-5,
    evaluation_strategy="epoch",
    save_strategy="epoch",
    load_best_model_at_end=True,
    metric_for_best_model="eval_loss",
    warmup_ratio=0.06,
    weight_decay=0.01,
)

trainer = WeightedTrainer(
    model=model, args=args,
    train_dataset=hf_train, eval_dataset=hf_valid,
)
trainer.train()
results = trainer.evaluate(hf_test)
print(results)

Model Benchmark Landscape

LIAR's 6-class task is genuinely difficult. Reported accuracies cluster in the 20–38% range, reflecting the fundamental hardness of distinguishing adjacent truthfulness gradations. Human performance on this task is estimated at around 57%, establishing an important ceiling. Reporting Best Practice for LIAR

Always report alongside exact accuracy: Mean Absolute Error (treating labels as ordered 0–5), off-by-one accuracy (prediction within 1 class of true label), and a confusion matrix heat map. A model with 30% exact accuracy but few extreme errors (pants-fire predicted as true) is far more practically useful than one with 32% exact accuracy that makes frequent extreme-class swaps.

8
Limitations & Ethical Considerations

What LIAR Cannot Tell You

!
US-Politics Domain Bias
LIAR is almost entirely US political discourse from 2007–2017. Models trained here transfer poorly to international politics, celebrity claims, scientific misinformation, or any post-2017 discourse patterns. Never treat LIAR accuracy as a proxy for general-purpose fake news detection.
!
Human Label Subjectivity
Each PolitiFact verdict involves editorial judgement. The boundary between barely-true and half-true — and between half-true and mostly-true — is inherently subjective. Inter-annotator agreement for adjacent classes is low, which is why human accuracy on this dataset is only ~57%.
!
Speaker Concentration Risk
~20 speakers account for a disproportionate share of statements. Models may learn "who is speaking" rather than "what is being said." Always ablate the speaker name field and evaluate with seen vs. unseen speaker partitions.
!
Temporal Validity
The political landscape of 2007–2017 differs significantly from today. Credit history vectors reflect historical rates, not current behavior. Treat LIAR as a closed benchmark, not a living dataset — it has not been updated since 2017.

Reading the Heatmap

The diagonal (top-left to bottom-right) shows correct predictions. Adjacent-class confusion is clearly dominant — the model most often errs by one step on the truth scale (pants-fire↔false, barely-true↔half-true, mostly-true↔true). Extreme confusions (pants-fire predicted as mostly-true or true) are rare, confirming the model has learned the ordinal structure even when exact labels fail. Use Mean Absolute Error (MAE) as your primary metric to properly reward proximity-correct predictions.
